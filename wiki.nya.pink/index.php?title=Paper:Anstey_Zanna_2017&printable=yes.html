Abstract

Parametrization of mesoscale eddy momentum fluxes based on large-scale flow deformation.

Suitable for use in eddy-permitting ocean general circulation models.

A primitive-equations model in an idealised double-gyre configuration at eddy-resolving horizontal resolution is used to diagnose the relationship between the proposed closure and the eddy fluxes resolved by the model. Favourable correlations suggest the closure could provide and appropriate deterministic parametrization of mesoscale eddies.

Different representations of the Reynolds stress tensor.

The parametrized forcing possesses the key quasi-geostrophic turbulence properties of energy conservation and enstrophy dissipation, and allows for upgradient fluxes leading to the sharpening of vorticity gradients.

The implementation of the closure for eddy-permitting ocean models requires only velocity derivatives and a single parameter that scales with model resolution.

Introduction

So the difference from Gent-McWilliams scheme is that that scheme is used for models that operate at scales at which mesoscale eddies are not resolved at all. I guess that scheme is not very suitable for partially-resolving models, and thus a new one is needed.

So, we are interested in models that operate at scales at which eddies are partially resolved.

"eddy-permitting spatial resolutions"

BEGIN

Eddy-permitting models are by definition truncated somewhere in the middle of the range of scales over which energy transfers occur.

AND

Eddy scales are close to the model grid scale

IMPLIES

Effects of parametrized eddy viscosity may be felt strongly within this range

END

Eddy viscosity is equivalent to downgradient flux. But in 2d models with jets there are also upgradient energy fluxes implemented by eddies. This, "eddy viscosity" is not nearly enough here.

Properties of parametrization that is being designed:

  1. Accounts for both upgradient and downgradient momentum fluxes.
  2. Respects the quasi-geostrophic turbulence properties:
    1. There are net upscale transfers of energy
    2. There are net downscale transfers of enstrophy
  3. Eddy-mean interaction leads to the maintenance of large-scale jets
  4. Parametrization should depend on the variability of partially resolved eddies present in the model (this dependence should have as few as possible adjustable parameters)

Ian Grooms et al. showed non-locality properties.

Ian Grooms and others showed that upgradient eddy fluxes in a quasi-geostrophic model could be modelled statistically by fitting autoregressive process to the eddy statistics obtained from an eddy resolving run, yielding an improved jet in a low-resolution model.

Jansen and others corrected spurious dissipation of kinetic energy by diagnosing the energy lost to gridscale viscosity and re-injecting this energy at larger spatial scales, so as to preserve energy conservation by mimicking the upscale cascade of energy that is expected in 2D turbulence.

Porta Mana and Zanna (2014) designed an eddy parametrization by determining a function of the coarse-grained flow in a high-resolution quasi-geostrophic model that correlated well with the eddy forcing to serve as the basis for a stochastic parametrization that depends on the resolved scales.

Rectified effects of upgradient momentum fluxes and energy backscatter (upscale energy transfer).

Macro-molecules are more likely to get tangled together at lower shears. Similarly, eddies are more likely to get tangled together at lower shears. On the other hand, eddies also create shear and deformation because they have specific velocities associated with them. All this supposedly happens only within some specific scale range (scale of deformation/shear has to be approximately the same as the scale of the eddies, I guess).

Eddy viscosity: analogy is made between random molecular motions and turbulent fluid motions.

Theory

Approach:

  • Parametrize the horizontal eddy Reynolds stress tensor directly.
  • The momentum and vorticity forcings due to parametrized eddies are then calculated from the stress tensor as flux divergences.
  • Parametrization satisfies conservation constraints if the relevant flux components vanish on the boundaries.

The parametrized Reynolds stress tensor will depend on the spatial gradients of velocity, and will be concisely expressed in terms of the flow deformation and the vorticity.

Form of the stress tensor

Consider a general stress tensor, T {\displaystyle T} :

T = [ a b c d ] {\displaystyle T={\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}}}
∇ := ( ∂ x , ∂ y ) {\displaystyle \nabla :=(\partial _{x},\partial _{y})}
∇ ⋅ T = ( ∂ x , ∂ y ) [ a b c d ] = [ ∂ x a + ∂ y c ∂ x b + ∂ y d ] = [ u f  (zonal velocity forcing)  v f  (meridional velocity forcing)  ] {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot T=(\partial _{x},\partial _{y}){\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}\partial _{x}a+\partial _{y}c\\\partial _{x}b+\partial _{y}d\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}u_{f}{\text{ (zonal velocity forcing) }}\\v_{f}{\text{ (meridional velocity forcing) }}\end{bmatrix}}}

In words: divergence of the stress tensor gives velocity vector forcing.

Assumption: An expression for T {\displaystyle T} can be found that approximates the effects of small-scale turbulence on the large-scale flow.

With respect to the assumption of 2D flow, although quasi-geostrophic turbulence (Charney, 1971) is "quasi-2D", we focus here on the simpler barotropic case.

Following Rivlin and Ericksen (1955), we assume T {\displaystyle T} can be expressed as a linear combination of tensors whose components are functions of the spatial gradients of velocity and their time derivatives.

The velocity gradient tensor is:

∇ u = [ u x u y v x v y ] {\displaystyle \nabla \mathbf {u} ={\begin{bmatrix}u_{x}&u_{y}\\v_{x}&v_{y}\end{bmatrix}}}

Symmetric and antisymmetric parts are referred to as strain and vorticity tensors:

  • Strain: S = 1 2 ( ∇ u + ∇ u T ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} ={\frac {1}{2}}(\nabla \mathbf {u} +\nabla \mathbf {u} ^{T})}
  • Vorticity: W = 1 2 ( ∇ u − ∇ u T ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {W} ={\frac {1}{2}}(\nabla \mathbf {u} -\nabla \mathbf {u} ^{T})}

Since a fluid stress tensor is required to be symmetric to satisfy angular momentum conservation, T {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} } can be written in the form:

T = [ a 0 0 a ] + [ b c c − b ] {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ={\begin{bmatrix}a&0\\0&a\end{bmatrix}}+{\begin{bmatrix}b&c\\c&-b\end{bmatrix}}}

Here a {\displaystyle a} is the mean of the diagonal entries, and b {\displaystyle b} actually lets us know the diagonal entries, as a function of the mean. Off-diagonal entries have to be the same, i.e. c {\displaystyle c} for the matrix (tensor, whatever; well, technically physical tensors have more properties than regular matrices, in fact they represent tensor fields) to be symmetric.

More compact form:

T = a I + b R + + c R × {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} =a\mathbf {I} +b\mathbf {R} _{+}+c\mathbf {R} _{\times }}

Here a {\displaystyle a} , b {\displaystyle b} , and c {\displaystyle c} are scalar valued functions.

We have:

  • Strain: S = 1 2 ( σ I + D ~ R + + D R × ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} ={\frac {1}{2}}(\sigma \mathbf {I} +{\tilde {D}}\mathbf {R} _{+}+D\mathbf {R} _{\times })}
  • Vorticity: W = 1 2 ζ R {\displaystyle \mathbf {W} ={\frac {1}{2}}\zeta \mathbf {R} }

Where:

ζ = v x − u y  (vorticity) {\displaystyle \zeta =v_{x}-u_{y}{\text{ (vorticity)}}}
σ = u x + v y  (divergence) {\displaystyle \sigma =u_{x}+v_{y}{\text{ (divergence)}}}
D ~ = u x − v y  stretching deformation {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}=u_{x}-v_{y}{\text{ stretching deformation}}}
D = u y + v x  shearing deformation {\displaystyle D=u_{y}+v_{x}{\text{ shearing deformation}}}

Notice that these are basically all possible combinations of velocity derivatives in some sense under some constraints.

Nonzero values of the fields D ~ {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}} and D {\displaystyle D} represent changes in the shape of a fluid parcel.

If D ~ > 0 {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}>0} :

  • Parcel is strethced in the x-direction and contracted in the y-direction.

Effect of D {\displaystyle D} is similar, but the stretching is aligned with axes rotated 45 ∘ {\displaystyle 45^{\circ }} from the x,y-axes.

Deformation frame:

  • Need an angle.
  • But deformation becomes a single scalar parameter δ {\displaystyle \delta } .
  • Invariant under rotation of the coordinate axes.

Notes:

  • δ {\displaystyle \delta } gives the fractional rate of change of the fluid parcel's aspect ratio
  • σ {\displaystyle \sigma } gives the fractional rate of change of the fluid parcel's area

Axis of dilation.

Note: Smagorinsky (1963) viscosity coefficient depends on the flow deformation.

Rivlin and Ericksen (1955) tensors:

A 1 = 2 S {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{1}=2\mathbf {S} }
A 2 = D A 1 D t + ∇ u T A 1 + A 1 ∇ u {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{2}={\frac {D\mathbf {A} _{1}}{Dt}}+\nabla \mathbf {u} ^{T}\mathbf {A} _{1}+\mathbf {A} _{1}\nabla \mathbf {u} }
⋯ {\displaystyle \cdots }
A m + 1 = D A m D t + ∇ u T A m + A m ∇ u {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{m+1}={\frac {D\mathbf {A} _{m}}{Dt}}+\nabla \mathbf {u} ^{T}\mathbf {A} _{m}+\mathbf {A} _{m}\nabla \mathbf {u} }

where

D D t := ∂ ∂ t + u ∂ ∂ x + v ∂ ∂ y {\displaystyle {\frac {D}{Dt}}:={\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}+u{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}+v{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}}

is the material derivative.

We can now define eddy viscosity T ( v ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ^{(v)}} , where:

T ( v ) := ν A 1 = 2 ν S {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ^{(v)}:=\nu \mathbf {A} _{1}=2\nu \mathbf {S} }

In general, ν > 0 {\displaystyle \nu >0} can be a fourth-order tensor, but we will assume it to be a constant coefficient.

This essentially gives a parametrization of eddy viscosity in terms of strain.

This only gives downgradient fluxes. To get upgradient fluxes, need further terms. For example, κ 2 A 2 {\displaystyle \kappa _{2}\mathbf {A} _{2}} .

Note: κ {\displaystyle \kappa } terms are assumed to be constant coefficients that play a role analogous to the eddy viscosity coefficient ν = κ 1 {\displaystyle \nu =\kappa _{1}} . But it's probably not actually constant, and varies depending on the dynamical regime. It's probably roughly proportional to the scale of eddies.

But this paper really wants it so that the functional dependence of T {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} } on the flow and on spatial location to be captured by A m {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{m}} , not by κ m {\displaystyle \kappa _{m}} .

Next, only spatial part of A 2 {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{2}} is considered:

A 2 = ∇ u T A 1 + A 1 ∇ {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{2}=\nabla \mathbf {u} ^{T}\mathbf {A} _{1}+\mathbf {A} _{1}\nabla }

This is because for simplicity, parametrization is being designed to only depend on the instantaneous state of the flow.

Time derivative could model for memory effects and there is evidence for that in other works.

Important result of this section:

S W − W S {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} \mathbf {W} -\mathbf {W} \mathbf {S} }

could be used to parametrize stuff.

In addition:

S W − W S = 1 2 ζ ( D R + − D ~ R × ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} \mathbf {W} -\mathbf {W} \mathbf {S} ={\frac {1}{2}}\zeta (D\mathbf {R} _{+}-{\tilde {D}}\mathbf {R} _{\times })}

Notice that this quantity on the left is basically a commutator of strain and vorticity tensors. And this commutator is proportional to vorticity and can be expressed as a linear combination of two rotation matrices).

Momentum and vorticity forcing

Candidate parametrized Reynolds stress tensor:

T ( κ ) = 2 κ ( S W − W S ) = κ ζ ( D R + − D ~ R × ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ^{(\kappa )}=2\kappa (\mathbf {S} \mathbf {W} -\mathbf {W} \mathbf {S} )=\kappa \zeta (D\mathbf {R} _{+}-{\tilde {D}}\mathbf {R} _{\times })}

κ {\displaystyle \kappa } has units of length 2 {\displaystyle {\text{length}}^{2}}

This gives:

  • Zonal momentum tendency: ∂ u ( κ ) ∂ t = κ [ ( ζ D ) x − ( ζ D ~ ) y ] {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial u^{(\kappa )}}{\partial t}}=\kappa \left[(\zeta D)_{x}-(\zeta {\tilde {D}})_{y}\right]}
  • Meridional momentum tendency: ∂ v ( κ ) ∂ t = − κ [ ( ζ D ) x + ( ζ D ~ ) x ] {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial v^{(\kappa )}}{\partial t}}=-\kappa \left[(\zeta D)_{x}+(\zeta {\tilde {D}})_{x}\right]}
  • Vorticity tendency: ∂ ζ ( κ ) ∂ t = − κ [ Δ 2 ( ζ D ~ ) + 2 ∂ x y ( ζ D ) ] {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta ^{(\kappa )}}{\partial t}}=-\kappa \left[\Delta ^{2}(\zeta {\tilde {D}})+2\partial _{xy}(\zeta D)\right]}

Can simplify vorticity tendency further for incompressible 2D flow ( σ = 0 {\displaystyle \sigma =0} ):

∂ ζ ( κ ) ∂ t = − κ ( D ~ Δ 2 ζ + 2 D ζ x y ) {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta ^{(\kappa )}}{\partial t}}=-\kappa ({\tilde {D}}\Delta ^{2}\zeta +2D\zeta _{xy})}

This equation represents the effects of a large-scale velocity field (that induces D ~ {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}} and D {\displaystyle D} ) acting to deform a smaller-scale vorticity field (via Δ 2 ζ {\displaystyle \Delta ^{2}\zeta } and 2 ζ x y {\displaystyle 2\zeta _{xy}} ).

Can interpret the forcing as a modified viscosity.

Total parametrized vorticity tendency:

∂ ζ ( ν , κ ) ∂ t = ν ∇ 2 ζ − κ ( D ~ Δ 2 ζ + 2 D ζ x y ) {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta ^{(\nu ,\kappa )}}{\partial t}}=\nu \nabla ^{2}\zeta -\kappa ({\tilde {D}}\Delta ^{2}\zeta +2D\zeta _{xy})}

Now go to the deformation frame. Can do this because the operator D ~ Δ 2 + 2 D ∂ x y {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}\Delta ^{2}+2D\partial _{xy}} is invariant under rotation. Same true for vorticity.

In this new frame:

∂ ζ ( ν , κ ) ∂ t = ν ( ζ x x + ζ y y ) − κ δ ( ζ x x − ζ y y ) = ( ν − κ δ ) ζ x x + ( ν + κ δ ) ζ y y {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta ^{(\nu ,\kappa )}}{\partial t}}=\nu (\zeta _{xx}+\zeta _{yy})-\kappa \delta (\zeta _{xx}-\zeta _{yy})=(\nu -\kappa \delta )\zeta _{xx}+(\nu +\kappa \delta )\zeta _{yy}}

δ > 0 {\displaystyle \delta >0} , thus the total parametrized forcing resembles a kind of anisotropic viscosity, with different viscosity coefficients acting in the directions parallel and perpendicular to the axis of dilation.

Whether the viscosity is enhanced or reduced depends on the magnitude of deformation, as well as the sign and magnitude of κ {\displaystyle \kappa } .

Energy and enstrophy

In models, eddy viscosity will tend to dissipate enstrophy at small scales, but it will also tend to give spurious loss of energy at small scales. Various parametrization schemes have been designed to counteract this effect.

Desirable properties: energy cascades to large scales, enstrophy cascades to small scales

Energy tendency (forcing):

∂ E ∂ t = ∇ ⋅ ( 2 ν S u ) − ν ( σ 2 + δ 2 ) {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial E}{\partial t}}=\nabla \cdot (2\nu \mathbf {S} \mathbf {u} )-\nu (\sigma ^{2}+\delta ^{2})}

Eddy viscosity is a sink of energy for ν > 0 {\displaystyle \nu >0} .

Since the energy forcing by T ( κ ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ^{(\kappa )}} is the divergence of a flux, its domain-integrated value depends on the flux at the boundaries.

Enstrophy tendency:

∂ Z ∂ t ( κ ) = 2 κ ∇ ζ ⋅ S ∇ ζ = − κ D D t | ∇ ζ | 2 + 2 κ ∇ ζ ⋅ ∇ D ζ D t {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial Z}{\partial t}}^{(\kappa )}=2\kappa \nabla \zeta \cdot \mathbf {S} \nabla \zeta =-\kappa {\frac {D}{Dt}}|\nabla \zeta |^{2}+2\kappa \nabla \zeta \cdot \nabla {\frac {D\zeta }{Dt}}}

Flux behaviour

δ {\displaystyle \delta } represents the effect of a larger-scale deforming flow on a smaller-scale patch of vorticity => Interaction between different scales of motion.

Interaction between different scaled (due to nonlinearity of the vorticity flux):

∂ ζ ∂ t ( κ ) + ∇ ⋅ ( κ δ R + ∇ ζ ) = 0 {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta }{\partial t}}^{(\kappa )}+\nabla \cdot (\kappa \delta \mathbf {R} _{+}\nabla \zeta )=0}

2D Fourier expansion of the streamfunction:

ψ ( x ) = ∑ k ψ k ( t ) exp ⁡ ( i k ⋅ x ) {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {x} )=\sum _{\mathbf {k} }\psi _{\mathbf {k} }(t)\exp(i\mathbf {k} \cdot \mathbf {x} )}

Note: this is actually quite interesting way to expand streamfunction. It motivates very well the choice of Gaussian convolution as a way to get large scale field. Also note that this can be modified to work at any point. So this is essentially a way to expand streamfunction about a point. Would be interesting to actually do that -- i.e. compute expansion of streamfunction locally at different points in the field. This should provide some theoretical motivation for the choice of optimal partitioning scale.

Vorticity tendency for a single Fourier mode with wavenumber k + p {\displaystyle \mathbf {k} +\mathbf {p} } is:

∂ ζ k + p ∂ t ( κ ) ∝ − 2 κ ( k 2 + l 2 ) [ p q ( k 2 − l 2 ) − k l ( p 2 − q 2 ) ] ψ k ψ p {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta _{\mathbf {k} +\mathbf {p} }}{\partial t}}^{(\kappa )}\propto -2\kappa (k^{2}+l^{2})\left[pq(k^{2}-l^{2})-kl(p^{2}-q^{2})\right]\psi _{\mathbf {k} }\psi _{\mathbf {p} }}

Where k = ( k , l ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {k} =(k,l)} and p = ( p , q ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =(p,q)} are the spatial wavevectors of the ζ {\displaystyle \zeta } and δ {\displaystyle \delta } terms, respectively.

Thus, nonzero vorticity tendency requires that k ≠ p {\displaystyle \mathbf {k} \neq \mathbf {p} } , implying interaction between different scales of motion.

T ( κ ) {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ^{(\kappa )}} can produce reasonable results if its input contains partially resolved eddies.

The qualitative agreement is not sensitive to the precise details of the eddy shape, as long as the eddy tilts with the shear.

The reason for agreement is found by plotting the x {\displaystyle x} , y {\displaystyle y} structure of u {\displaystyle u} , v {\displaystyle v} , ζ {\displaystyle \zeta } , D ~ {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}} and D {\displaystyle D} : the spatial phase relationship between u {\displaystyle u} and v {\displaystyle v} is similar to that between ζ {\displaystyle \zeta } and D ~ {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}} (and D {\displaystyle D}  ?).

=> Correlations are similar (when zonally averaged).

So, here is a comment: Can we optimize the averaging function so as to maximize the correlation? This is in a sense a way to more accurately pick different Fourier modes & analyze their relationship.

=> Impact of T {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} } on a large-scale zonal jet would be qualitatively similar to that of the true Reynolds stresses, provided the resolved-flow spatial structures of ζ {\displaystyle \zeta } , D ~ {\displaystyle {\tilde {D}}} , D {\displaystyle D} are similar enough to the spatial structures of the unresolved eddies (which can be further optimized by picking the correct averaging function).

Diagnostics

Equation of motion for vorticity in the high resolution model:

∂ ζ f ∂ t + u f ⋅ ∇ f ζ f + β v f = F f + ν f ∇ f 2 ζ f {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta _{f}}{\partial t}}+\mathbf {u} _{f}\cdot \nabla _{f}\zeta _{f}+\beta v_{f}=F_{f}+\nu _{f}\nabla _{f}^{2}\zeta _{f}}

Subscript f {\displaystyle f} denotes the fine-grain (i.e. small-scale) model.

Coarse-grain by averaging the fine-grain model:

∂ ζ f ¯ ∂ t + u f ⋅ ∇ f ζ f ¯ + β v f ¯ = F f ¯ + ν f ∇ f 2 ζ f ¯ {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial {\overline {\zeta _{f}}}}{\partial t}}+{\overline {\mathbf {u} _{f}\cdot \nabla _{f}\zeta _{f}}}+\beta {\overline {v_{f}}}={\overline {F_{f}}}+\nu _{f}{\overline {\nabla _{f}^{2}\zeta _{f}}}}

The ideal behaviour of a hypothetical lower-resolution model is that it mimics (statistically) the evolution given by ∂ ζ f ¯ ∂ t {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial {\overline {\zeta _{f}}}}{\partial t}}} :

∂ ζ c ∂ t + u c ⋅ ∇ c ζ c + β v c = F c + ν c ∇ c 2 ζ c + S ζ {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \zeta _{c}}{\partial t}}+\mathbf {u} _{c}\cdot \nabla _{c}\zeta _{c}+\beta v_{c}=F_{c}+\nu _{c}\nabla _{c}^{2}\zeta _{c}+S_{\zeta }}

Viscosity of a lower resolution model is necessarily larger than the smallest value allowable in the fine-grain model: ν c > ν f {\displaystyle \nu _{c}>\nu _{f}} .
