We try to solve a convex optimization problem Equation 1 by instead looking for a solution for a non-convex optimization problem Equation 2. It turns out that these two problems actually have the same solution, as is shown in Hastie et al.'s paper.

minimize M H ( M ) := 1 2 | | P Ω ( X − M ) | F 2 + λ | | M | | ∗ (convex) {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&{\underset {M}{\text{minimize}}}&&H(M):={\frac {1}{2}}||P_{\Omega }(X-M)|_{F}^{2}+\lambda ||M||_{*}\end{aligned}}\quad {\text{(convex)}}} (1)
minimize A , B F ( A , B ) := 1 2 | | P Ω ( X − A B T ) | | F 2 + λ 2 | | A | | F 2 + λ 2 | | B | | F 2 (non-convex) {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&{\underset {A,B}{\text{minimize}}}&&F(A,B):={\frac {1}{2}}||P_{\Omega }(X-AB^{T})||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||A||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||B||_{F}^{2}\end{aligned}}\quad {\text{(non-convex)}}} (2)

To try to get a solution for the same problem, we iteratively freeze one of the variables, and solve for the other one (since in this case, the problem becomes convex at each iteration).

But we don't solve it directly. Instead we define surrogate functions and optimize them instead:

Q A ∗ ( Z 1 | A , B ) := 1 2 | | P Ω ( X − Z 1 B T ) + P Ω ⊥ ( A B T − Z 1 B T ) ∘ 1 K | | F 2 + λ 2 | | Z 1 | | F 2 + λ 2 | | B | | F 2 {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z_{1}|A,B):={\frac {1}{2}}||P_{\Omega }(X-Z_{1}B^{T})+P_{\Omega }^{\bot }(AB^{T}-Z_{1}B^{T})\circ {\frac {1}{K}}||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||Z_{1}||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||B||_{F}^{2}} (3)
Q B ∗ ( Z 2 | A , B ) := 1 2 | | P Ω ( X − A Z 2 T ) + P Ω ⊥ ( A B T − A Z 2 T ) ∘ 1 K | | F 2 + λ 2 | | A | | F 2 + λ 2 | | Z 2 | | F 2 {\displaystyle Q_{B}^{*}(Z_{2}|A,B):={\frac {1}{2}}||P_{\Omega }(X-AZ_{2}^{T})+P_{\Omega }^{\bot }(AB^{T}-AZ_{2}^{T})\circ {\frac {1}{K}}||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||A||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||Z_{2}||_{F}^{2}} (4)

These surrogate functions have important properties:

  • Q A ∗ ( Z 1 | A , B ) ≥ F ( Z 1 , B ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z_{1}|A,B)\geq F(Z_{1},B)} , and Q B ∗ ( Z 2 | A , B ) ≥ F ( A , Z 2 ) {\displaystyle Q_{B}^{*}(Z_{2}|A,B)\geq F(A,Z_{2})} (5)
  • Q A ∗ ( A | A , B ) = F ( A , B ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(A|A,B)=F(A,B)} , and Q B ∗ ( B | A , B ) = F ( A , B ) {\displaystyle Q_{B}^{*}(B|A,B)=F(A,B)}

Convergence

  • η k := 1 2 ( | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) B k T | | F 2 + | | A k + 1 ( B k + 1 − B k ) T | | F 2 ) + λ 2 ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle \eta _{k}:={\frac {1}{2}}(||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})B_{k}^{T}||_{F}^{2}+||A_{k+1}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})^{T}||_{F}^{2})+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})}
  • F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k + 1 ) ≥ η k {\displaystyle F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k+1})\geq \eta _{k}}
  • η k = 0 {\displaystyle \eta _{k}=0} iff A k , B k {\displaystyle A_{k},B_{k}} is a fixed point
  • The decreasing sequence of objective values F ( A k , B k ) {\displaystyle F(A_{k},B_{k})} converges to F ∞ ≥ 0 {\displaystyle F^{\infty }\geq 0} and η k → 0 {\displaystyle \eta _{k}\rightarrow 0} . Furthermore:
min 1 ≤ k ≤ K η k ≤ ( F ( A 1 , B 1 ) − F ∞ ) ) / K {\displaystyle \min _{1\leq k\leq K}\eta _{k}\leq (F(A_{1},B_{1})-F^{\infty }))/K} . Derivation of η k {\displaystyle \eta _{k}}

  1. F ( A k , B k ) = Q A ∗ ( A k | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle F(A_{k},B_{k})=Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k}|A_{k},B_{k})} .
  2. F ( A k + 1 , B k ) ≤ Q A ∗ ( A k + 1 | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle F(A_{k+1},B_{k})\leq Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k+1}|A_{k},B_{k})} , since the second one simply has some squared terms added here and there.
  3. A k + 1 = argmin Z Q A ∗ ( Z | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle A_{k+1}={\text{argmin}}_{Z}Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A_{k},B_{k})} . In particular, this means that first derivative of Q A ∗ ( Z | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A_{k},B_{k})} with respect to Z {\displaystyle Z} is zero.

Now, to proceed we simply need to compute second derivative of Q A ∗ ( Z | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A_{k},B_{k})} with respect to Z {\displaystyle Z} . Notice that Q A ∗ ( Z | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A_{k},B_{k})} is a quadratic function in Z {\displaystyle Z} , so it can be expressed exactly as Taylor series with three terms (where the second term, corresponding to first derivative, will be zero at A k + 1 {\displaystyle A_{k+1}} ).

Surrogate function in index notation is:

Q A ∗ ( Z | A , B ) = 1 2 Z i k B T k j Ω i j Z i k B T k j Ω i j + 1 2 Z i k B T k j Ω i j ⊥ 1 K i j Z i k B T k j ( Ω ⊥ ) i j 1 K i j + λ 2 Z i j Z i j + λ 2 B i j B i j + 1 2 X i j ∗ X ∗ i j − X i j ∗ Z i k B T k j Ω i j − X i j ∗ Z i k B T k j ( Ω ⊥ ) i j 1 K i j {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A,B){}&={\frac {1}{2}}{Z_{i}}^{k}{B^{T}}_{kj}\Omega _{ij}Z^{ik}{{B^{T}}_{k}}^{j}\Omega ^{ij}\\{}&+{\frac {1}{2}}{Z_{i}}^{k}{B^{T}}_{kj}\Omega _{ij}^{\bot }{\frac {1}{K_{ij}}}Z^{ik}{{B^{T}}_{k}}^{j}(\Omega ^{\bot })^{ij}{\frac {1}{K^{ij}}}\\{}&+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}Z_{ij}Z^{ij}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}B_{ij}B^{ij}+{\frac {1}{2}}X_{ij}^{*}X^{*ij}\\{}&-X_{ij}^{*}Z^{ik}{{B^{T}}_{k}}^{j}\Omega ^{ij}-X_{ij}^{*}Z^{ik}{{B^{T}}_{k}}^{j}(\Omega ^{\bot })^{ij}{\frac {1}{K^{ij}}}\end{aligned}}}

Lets differentiate it:

Let's compute second derivatives:

∂ 2 Q A ∗ ( Z | A , B ) ∂ Z x y ∂ Z a b = δ x a δ y b ( B T b j Ω a j B T b j Ω a j + B T b j ( Ω ⊥ ) a j 1 K a j B T b j ( Ω ⊥ ) a j 1 K a j + λ ) {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A,B)}{\partial Z_{xy}\partial Z_{ab}}}=\delta _{xa}\delta _{yb}({{B^{T}}^{b}}_{j}{\Omega ^{a}}_{j}{B^{T}}^{bj}{\Omega _{a}}^{j}+{{B^{T}}^{b}}_{j}{(\Omega ^{\bot })^{a}}_{j}{\frac {1}{{K^{a}}_{j}}}{B^{T}}^{bj}{(\Omega ^{\bot })_{a}}^{j}{\frac {1}{{K_{a}}^{j}}}+\lambda )}

Now, Q A ∗ ( Z | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A_{k},B_{k})} is a quadratic function, so we have an exact expression using a second order Taylor Series:

Q A ∗ ( A k + 1 | A k , B k ) = Q A ∗ ( A k | A k , B k ) + ∂ Q A ∗ ( A k + 1 | A , B ) ∂ Z a b ( A k + 1 − A k ) a b + 1 2 ∂ 2 Q A ∗ ( Z | A , B ) ∂ Z x y ∂ Z a b ( A k + 1 − A k ) x y ( A k + 1 − A k ) a b {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k+1}|A_{k},B_{k})=Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k}|A_{k},B_{k})+{\frac {\partial Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k+1}|A,B)}{\partial Z_{ab}}}(A_{k+1}-A_{k})^{ab}+{\frac {1}{2}}{\frac {\partial ^{2}Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A,B)}{\partial Z_{xy}\partial Z_{ab}}}(A_{k+1}-A_{k})^{xy}(A_{k+1}-A_{k})^{ab}}

Since A k + 1 {\displaystyle A_{k+1}} is an extrema of Q A ∗ ( Z | A k , B k ) {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A_{k},B_{k})} , second derivative vanishes and we are left with:

Q A ∗ ( A k + 1 | A k , B k ) − Q A ∗ ( A k | A k , B k ) = 1 2 ∂ 2 Q A ∗ ( Z | A , B ) ∂ Z x y ∂ Z a b ( A k + 1 − A k ) x y ( A k + 1 − A k ) a b {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k+1}|A_{k},B_{k})-Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k}|A_{k},B_{k})={\frac {1}{2}}{\frac {\partial ^{2}Q_{A}^{*}(Z|A,B)}{\partial Z_{xy}\partial Z_{ab}}}(A_{k+1}-A_{k})^{xy}(A_{k+1}-A_{k})^{ab}}

Now plug in the second derivative:

Let's play with it:

  • ( ( A k + 1 − A k ) B T ) a j B T b j = ( ( A k + 1 − A k ) B T ) a j B j b = ( ( A k + 1 − A k ) B T B ) a b {\displaystyle ((A_{k+1}-A_{k})B^{T})_{aj}{B^{T}}^{bj}=((A_{k+1}-A_{k})B^{T})_{aj}{B}^{jb}={((A_{k+1}-A_{k})B^{T}B)_{a}}^{b}}

Well, essentially the main idea here is that each row of ( A k + 1 − A k ) {\displaystyle (A_{k+1}-A_{k})} gets its own B {\displaystyle B} :

Q A ∗ ( A k + 1 | A k , B k ) − Q A ∗ ( A k | A k , B k ) = 1 2 ∑ a ∈ rows(A) | | ( A k + 1 − A k ) a B a T | | F 2 + λ 2 | | A k + 1 − A k | | F 2 {\displaystyle Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k+1}|A_{k},B_{k})-Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k}|A_{k},B_{k})={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{a\in {\text{rows(A)}}}||(A_{k+1}-A_{k})_{a}B_{a}^{T}||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||A_{k+1}-A_{k}||_{F}^{2}}

In words: We sum over rows of A {\displaystyle A} . For each row, we have a corresponding B a {\displaystyle B_{a}} matrix, which is basically B {\displaystyle B} weighted for unknown entries by the debiaser.


From this, we get:

F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k ) ≥ Q A ∗ ( A k | A k , B k ) − Q A ∗ ( A k + 1 | A k , B k ) = 1 2 ∑ a ∈ rows(A) | | ( A k + 1 − A k ) a B a T | | F 2 + λ 2 | | A k + 1 − A k | | F 2 {\displaystyle F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k})\geq Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k}|A_{k},B_{k})-Q_{A}^{*}(A_{k+1}|A_{k},B_{k})={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{a\in {\text{rows(A)}}}||(A_{k+1}-A_{k})_{a}B_{a}^{T}||_{F}^{2}+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}||A_{k+1}-A_{k}||_{F}^{2}}

Rates of convergence (AKA Corollary 1)

Now that we have an accurate expression for η k {\displaystyle \eta _{k}} , we can go on with studying rates of convergence.

Ok, first of all, lets assume that u I ⪰ B T B ⪰ l I {\displaystyle u\mathbf {I} \succeq B^{T}B\succeq l\mathbf {I} } and u I ⪰ A T A ⪰ l I {\displaystyle u\mathbf {I} \succeq A^{T}A\succeq l\mathbf {I} } . Where u {\displaystyle u} and l {\displaystyle l} are constants independent of k {\displaystyle k} .

For simplicity, lets study rank 1 case first. In this case, u ≥ B T B ≥ l {\displaystyle u\geq B^{T}B\geq l} and u ≥ A T A ≥ l {\displaystyle u\geq A^{T}A\geq l} .

In this case, rows of A {\displaystyle A} and B {\displaystyle B} will be just scalars.

Ok, lets partition A {\displaystyle A} (or B {\displaystyle B} ) into measured (a) and unknown (b) entries:

[ a | b ] {\displaystyle {\begin{bmatrix}a&|&b\end{bmatrix}}}


Then, say A T A = a T a + b T b {\displaystyle A^{T}A=a^{T}a+b^{T}b} and A ∗ T A ∗ = a T a + ( b ∘ 1 K a ) ) T ( b ∘ 1 K a ) {\displaystyle {A^{*}}^{T}A^{*}=a^{T}a+(b\circ {\frac {1}{K_{a}}}))^{T}(b\circ {\frac {1}{K_{a}}})} . Re-write in index notation:

A ∗ T A ∗ = a T a + b i b i 1 K a i 2 {\displaystyle {A^{*}}^{T}A^{*}=a^{T}a+b_{i}b^{i}{\frac {1}{{K_{a}}_{i}^{2}}}}

Now, subtract non-debiased quantity from the debiased one:

A ∗ T A ∗ − A T A = b i b i ( 1 K a i 2 − 1 ) {\displaystyle {A^{*}}^{T}A^{*}-A^{T}A=b_{i}b^{i}({\frac {1}{{K_{a}}_{i}^{2}}}-1)}

Now, let's suppose that debiaser is a scalar quantity:

A ∗ T A ∗ = a T a + b i b i 1 K a 2 {\displaystyle {A^{*}}^{T}A^{*}=a^{T}a+b_{i}b^{i}{\frac {1}{{K_{a}}^{2}}}}

And:

A ∗ T A ∗ − A T A = b T b ( 1 K a 2 − 1 ) {\displaystyle {A^{*}}^{T}A^{*}-A^{T}A=b^{T}b({\frac {1}{{K_{a}}^{2}}}-1)}

Oh, yes. Also, in rank one case, u = B T B = l {\displaystyle u=B^{T}B=l} and u = A T A = l {\displaystyle u=A^{T}A=l} since we are dealing with scalars. So we might as well set u = l = p {\displaystyle u=l=p} .

We can then say that there is some optimal value for p {\displaystyle p} , which we would call p d {\displaystyle p_{d}} .

We would call the non-debiased p {\displaystyle p} as p o {\displaystyle p_{o}} , and debiased one as p d {\displaystyle p_{d}} .

Debiasing

Let's revisit

F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k + 1 ) ≥ 1 2 ( ∑ a ∈ rows(A) | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) a B k T a | | F 2 + ∑ a ∈ rows(B) | | A k + 1 a ( B k + 1 − B k ) a T | | F 2 ) + λ 2 ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k+1})\geq {\frac {1}{2}}(\sum _{a\in {\text{rows(A)}}}||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})_{a}{B_{k}^{T}}_{a}||_{F}^{2}+\sum _{a\in {\text{rows(B)}}}||{A_{k+1}}_{a}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})_{a}^{T}||_{F}^{2})+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})}

We can think of both left and right side as random variables which depend on the sampling density. Suppose we have a uniform sampling density.

Since these are random variables, we can compute means: Let's revisit

E ( F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k + 1 ) ) ≥ 1 2 E ( ∑ a ∈ rows(A) | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) a B k T a | | F 2 + ∑ a ∈ rows(B) | | A k + 1 a ( B k + 1 − B k ) a T | | F 2 ) + λ 2 ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} (F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k+1}))\geq {\frac {1}{2}}\mathbb {E} (\sum _{a\in {\text{rows(A)}}}||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})_{a}{B_{k}^{T}}_{a}||_{F}^{2}+\sum _{a\in {\text{rows(B)}}}||{A_{k+1}}_{a}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})_{a}^{T}||_{F}^{2})+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})}

There's no error: second term on the right hand side is constant with respect to sampling density.

Now, to continue, we need to compute E | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) a B k T a | | F 2 {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})_{a}{B_{k}^{T}}_{a}||_{F}^{2}} . In rank=1 case, ( A k − A k + 1 ) {\displaystyle (A_{k}-A_{k+1})} is just a scalar, and B k T a {\displaystyle {B_{k}^{T}}_{a}} is a vector.

We then have:

| | ( A k − A k + 1 ) a B k T a | | F 2 = ∑ i , a  measured [ ( A k − A k + 1 ) i B k i T ] 2 + ∑ i , a  not measured [ ( A k − A k + 1 ) i B k i T 1 k ] 2 {\displaystyle ||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})_{a}{B_{k}^{T}}_{a}||_{F}^{2}=\sum _{i,a{\text{ measured}}}[(A_{k}-A_{k+1})^{i}B_{ki}^{T}]^{2}+\sum _{i,a{\text{ not measured}}}[(A_{k}-A_{k+1})^{i}B_{ki}^{T}{\frac {1}{k}}]^{2}}

If we have uniform probability, an we measure with probability p {\displaystyle p} , and don't measure with probability 1 − p {\displaystyle 1-p} , we have:

E | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) a B k T a | | F 2 = ∑ i [ ( A k − A k + 1 ) i B k i T ] 2 ∗ p + ∑ i [ ( A k − A k + 1 ) i B k i T ] 2 ∗ ( 1 − p ) k 2 {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})_{a}{B_{k}^{T}}_{a}||_{F}^{2}=\sum _{i}[(A_{k}-A_{k+1})^{i}B_{ki}^{T}]^{2}*p+\sum _{i}[(A_{k}-A_{k+1})^{i}B_{ki}^{T}]^{2}*{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}}}

Or:

E | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) a B k T a | | F 2 = ∑ i [ ( A k − A k + 1 ) i B k i T ] 2 ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ||(A_{k}-A_{k+1})_{a}{B_{k}^{T}}_{a}||_{F}^{2}=\sum _{i}[(A_{k}-A_{k+1})^{i}B_{ki}^{T}]^{2}*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})}

Plug this back in the original expression to get:

E ( F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k + 1 ) ) ≥ 1 2 ( | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) B k T | | F 2 + | | A k + 1 ( B k + 1 − B k ) T | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ 2 ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} (F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k+1}))\geq {\frac {1}{2}}(||(A_{k}-A_{k+1}){B_{k}^{T}}||_{F}^{2}+||{A_{k+1}}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})^{T}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+{\frac {\lambda }{2}}(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})}

Now, on the average, we want left hand side to be zero (i.e. each iteration, we on the average get the correct result). That's why it's called debiasing in the first place.

If we hope for the left hand side to be zero, right hand side should also be zero:

0 = ( | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) B k T | | F 2 + | | A k + 1 ( B k + 1 − B k ) T | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle 0=(||(A_{k}-A_{k+1}){B_{k}^{T}}||_{F}^{2}+||{A_{k+1}}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})^{T}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda (||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})}

Now we can use the assumption that u I ⪰ B T B ⪰ l I {\displaystyle u\mathbf {I} \succeq B^{T}B\succeq l\mathbf {I} } and u I ⪰ A T A ⪰ l I {\displaystyle u\mathbf {I} \succeq A^{T}A\succeq l\mathbf {I} } to move matrix multiplications outside norms:

u ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) ≥ ( | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) B k T | | F 2 + | | A k + 1 ( B k + 1 − B k ) T | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) ≥ l ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}u(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda (||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})\geq \\(||(A_{k}-A_{k+1}){B_{k}^{T}}||_{F}^{2}+||{A_{k+1}}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})^{T}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda (||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})\\\geq l(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda (||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})\end{aligned}}}

In the rank=1 case, l = u = t {\displaystyle l=u=t} , and we get:

( | | ( A k − A k + 1 ) B k T | | F 2 + | | A k + 1 ( B k + 1 − B k ) T | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) = t ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) ∗ ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ ( | | A k − A k + 1 | | F 2 + | | B k + 1 − B k | | F 2 ) {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(||(A_{k}-A_{k+1}){B_{k}^{T}}||_{F}^{2}+||{A_{k+1}}(B_{k+1}-B_{k})^{T}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda (||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})=\\t(||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})*(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda (||A_{k}-A_{k+1}||_{F}^{2}+||B_{k+1}-B_{k}||_{F}^{2})\end{aligned}}}

From this, we get that:

t ( p + ( 1 − p ) k 2 ) + λ = 0 {\displaystyle t(p+{\frac {(1-p)}{k^{2}}})+\lambda =0}

And thus: k 2 = − 1 − p λ t + p = − t 1 − p λ + p t {\displaystyle k^{2}=-{\frac {1-p}{{\frac {\lambda }{t}}+p}}=-t{\frac {1-p}{\lambda +pt}}}

The case λ t → 0 {\displaystyle {\frac {\lambda }{t}}\to 0} is especially interesting, since expression simplifies to k = − 1 − p p {\displaystyle k=-{\frac {1-p}{p}}} , and there is no dependence on iterates or regularization parameter at all.

Note: it actually doesn't make sense for the mean to be zero. Instead it will be a small positive number, say ϵ {\displaystyle \epsilon } , and we would get:

k 2 = 1 − p ϵ − λ t − p {\displaystyle k^{2}={\frac {1-p}{{\frac {\epsilon -\lambda }{t}}-p}}}

This is actually a more sensible value for a debiaser as now its squared value doesn't have to be negative. All we need for that is ϵ − λ t − p > 0 {\displaystyle {\frac {\epsilon -\lambda }{t}}-p>0} , or:

ϵ − λ t > p {\displaystyle {\frac {\epsilon -\lambda }{t}}>p}

Since the mean is roughly proportional to t {\displaystyle t} , this should hold true in practice.

Discussion

  • This probably also carries on to rank>1 case easily.
  • Can extend to non-uniform probability by having a separate p for every row of A and B. But need to re-derive expression for those cases.
  • Can further analyze convergence rate, using Corollary 1
  • Lambda is independent of the dimensionality of the problem, right? In this case, λ t → 0 {\displaystyle {\frac {\lambda }{t}}\to 0} for any large problems as t depends on the size of the problem.
  • Can we reformulate Corollary 1 in probabilistic fashion? I.e. we could be able to estimate the unknown bounding parameters on eigenvalues by treating matrices as random variables.
  • At the very least, we can "treat the sampling pattern as a random variable".
  • We can use expectation values to show what the convergence rate is on the average, and then use "concentration inequalities" to show with what probability those convergence rates are guaranteed. I.e. "compute means and svd".

Furthermore:

  • It is strange to assume that E ( F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k + 1 ) ) = 0 {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} (F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k+1}))=0} , since the left hand side is always positive. All we can assume is that the mean is small. Though, we would definitely want the lower bound to be zero. And the higher bound can be used to estimate the convergence rate. But to be more accurate, should use concentration inequality.

Concentration

We want to compute

P [ F ( A k , B k ) − F ( A k + 1 , B k + 1 ) < ϵ ] {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} [F(A_{k},B_{k})-F(A_{k+1},B_{k+1})<\epsilon ]}

In words, this would tell us how close we are to a stationary point at k {\displaystyle k} th iteration.

So my idea is to bound this difference in terms of surrogate functions. Than we would have a different probability inequality that would be a lower bound for this one. But this second inequality would be expressible in terms of the differences of latent vectors. This is just something to think about. Not sure if this actually leads to useful results.

Another concentration inequality we might want to compute is:

P [ ( F ( A 1 , B 1 ) − F ∞ ) ) / K < ϵ ] {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} \left[\left(F(A_{1},B_{1})-F^{\infty })\right)/K<\epsilon \right]}

This should give the overall convergence rate in terms of probability density? Maybe read the other two papers? They might have more work in this framework.

More on debiasing

I think it could make sense to debias E A k {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} A_{k}} and E B k {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} B_{k}} . This would probably be more in hand with Yaniv et al.'s paper.

Probably won't be that hard at least in rank=1 case.

Even more, can do E X k = E ( A k B k T ) {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} X_{k}=\mathbb {E} (A_{k}{B_{k}}^{T})} .

Ok, let's try it in rank=1 case. In this case, A k + 1 a {\displaystyle {A_{k+1}}a} and A k a {\displaystyle {A_{k}}a} are just scalars.

A k + 1 a = ( A k a B i B i Ω a ⊥ i H + X i B i Ω a i ) / ( B i B i Ω a ⊥ i H + B i B i Ω a i + λ ) {\displaystyle {A_{k+1}}_{a}=({A_{k}}_{a}B_{i}B^{i}{\Omega _{a}^{\bot }}^{i}H+X_{i}B^{i}\Omega _{a}^{i})/(B_{i}B^{i}{\Omega _{a}^{\bot }}^{i}H+B_{i}B^{i}\Omega _{a}^{i}+\lambda )}

Here Ω a i {\displaystyle \Omega _{a}^{i}} is a vector that has all entries zero except for those ( a , i ) {\displaystyle (a,i)} that are measured. Let's assume that they are measured with uniform probability p {\displaystyle p} .

Also, here H = 1 K 2 {\displaystyle H={\frac {1}{K^{2}}}} is inverse of squared scalar debiaser.
