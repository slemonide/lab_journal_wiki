Reduction: basic concepts

K {\displaystyle K} a field n {\displaystyle n} , a positive integer

A binary form of degree n {\displaystyle n} over K {\displaystyle K} is a homogenous polynomial in K [ X , Y ] {\displaystyle K[X,Y]} of degree n {\displaystyle n} . The group G L ( 2 , K ) {\displaystyle GL(2,K)} acts on K [ X , Y ] {\displaystyle K[X,Y]} via linear substitutions of the form:

( α β γ δ ) : f ( X , Y ) ↦ f ( α X + β Y , γ X + δ Y ) {\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}\alpha &\beta \\\gamma &\delta \end{pmatrix}}:f(X,Y)\mapsto f(\alpha X+\beta Y,\gamma X+\delta Y)}

We are mainly concerned with the action of S L ( 2 , K ) {\displaystyle SL(2,K)} , specifically restricting K {\displaystyle K} to the ring of integers in our field.

For the inhomogeneous polynomial f ( X ) := f ( X , 1 ) {\displaystyle f(X):=f(X,1)} , the group action is simplified to:

f ( X ) ↦ ( γ X + δ ) n f ( α X + β γ X + δ ) {\displaystyle f(X)\mapsto (\gamma X+\delta )^{n}f\left({\frac {\alpha X+\beta }{\gamma X+\delta }}\right)}

Remarks on reduction over (general) number fields

Reduction of integer polynomials use the real embedding, Q ↪ R {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} \hookrightarrow \mathbb {R} } . However for a general number field K {\displaystyle K} we must use all the real embeddings of K {\displaystyle K} as well as those of the complex embeddings if K {\displaystyle K} is not totally real!

Also, must combine the bounds from the embeddings to obtain usable bounds and determine a finite search region for coefficients of reduced polynomials.

For the real quadratic fields of class 1, the real embeddings must be considered not separately, but simultaneously.

What are the possible embeddings of other number fields?

In an imaginary quadratic field K {\displaystyle K} , instead of reduction by means of points in the upper half-plane, we use the Hermitian quadratics which have the form

h ( z , w ) = a z z ¯ + b z w ¯ + b ¯ z ¯ w + c w w ¯ {\displaystyle h(z,w)=az{\bar {z}}+bz{\bar {w}}+{\bar {b}}{\bar {z}}w+cw{\bar {w}}}

where a , c ∈ R {\displaystyle a,c\in \mathbb {R} } and b ∈ C {\displaystyle b\in \mathbb {C} } . Instead of points on the upper half-plane, these are points in hyperbolic 3-space H 3 {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}_{3}} .

The modular group S L ( 2 , O K ) {\displaystyle SL(2,{\mathcal {O}}_{K})} is called the Bianchi group and acts on both the set of hermitian quadratics and on H 3 {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}_{3}} . Read the works of Bianchi, Humbert, etc and Julia 1917
